# Malleable Software: Restoring User Agency in a World of Locked-Down Apps

**Authors:** Geoffrey Litt, Josh Horowitz, Peter van Hardenberg, Todd Matthews  
**Publication Date:** June 2025  
**Source:** Ink & Switch

## Overview

This comprehensive essay presents a vision for computing systems that empower users to reshape their digital tools rather than passively accept rigid, mass-produced applications.

## Core Argument

The authors contend that modern software has become unnecessarily inflexible. They write: "When your tools don't work the way you need them to, you submit feedback and hope for the best." This creates a situation where users adapt their workflows to fit software, inverting the ideal relationship.

The essay proposes "malleable software"—a computing ecosystem where "anyone can adapt their tools to their needs with minimal friction." This represents a fundamental shift from treating users as passive consumers to recognizing them as active co-creators.

## Key Design Principles

**1. Gentle Slope from User to Creator**

Rather than forcing a choice between passive use or complete programming expertise, the authors advocate for graduated customization pathways. Spreadsheets exemplify this principle: users can start by viewing cells, progress to editing formulas, and eventually create complex calculations—each step requiring incrementally more skill.

**2. Tools Over Applications**

The essay uses an apt metaphor: applications function like single-purpose kitchen gadgets (avocado slicers), while malleable systems should operate like versatile knives. Composable tools sharing data enable flexible workflows that span multiple specialized utilities rather than forcing users into monolithic applications.

**3. Communal Creation**

Malleability needn't be purely individualistic. Local communities—departments, teams, organizations—can collectively build and maintain software serving their specific needs, supported by "local developers" who guide colleagues up the customization slope.

## Limitations of Existing Approaches

The authors systematically examine current customization methods:

- **Settings** remain constrained by developer foresight
- **Plugins** require authorized extension points
- **Modding** demands reverse-engineering and lacks cross-application compatibility
- **Open source** access alone doesn't guarantee low-friction modification
- **AI assistance** generates applications but doesn't solve underlying structural problems with siloed, closed ecosystems

## Ink & Switch Prototypes

The research team developed several experimental systems:

**Infrastructure Projects:**
- **PushPin**: A collaborative canvas using document-functional reactive programming
- **Cambria**: Mediates schema compatibility between tools through live data translation
- **Farm**: Stores source code in synchronized Automerge documents
- **Patchwork**: Adds version control and bootstrapping capabilities; the authors wrote this essay within Patchwork itself

**Dynamic Document Projects:**
- **Potluck**: Enriches plaintext notes with computed behavior
- **Embark**: Hierarchical outlines for travel planning with embedded maps and calendars

## Supporting Infrastructure

Success requires reconsidering multiple computing layers:

- **Data sharing** through common formats (filesystem analogue)
- **Runtime editability** allowing code modification without redeployment
- **UI composition** permitting multiple editors on shared data
- **Version control** designed for casual collaboration, not just professional teams

## Remaining Challenges

The authors acknowledge substantial obstacles:

- **Privacy and security** with untrusted modifications accessing sensitive data
- **Business models** for sustainable development of composable tools
- **Cultural shifts** fostering user agency and creative participation
- **Technical architecture** supporting flexible UI composition and tool interoperability

## Historical Context

The essay draws inspiration from decades of research and practice: Alan Kay's vision of software as malleable clay, HyperCard's levels system, Smalltalk's object-oriented substrate, spreadsheets' gentle customization slope, and desktop filesystems enabling tool composition.

## Broader Implications

The authors argue this represents not merely technical improvement but a philosophical reorientation. Computing should support what physical spaces naturally provide: the capacity to gradually reshape environments reflecting individual and community needs.

They conclude: "Everyone deserves the right to evolve their digital environments. It's an important way to fulfill our creative potential and maintain a sense of agency in a world that is increasingly defined in code."
