# Malleable Software: Restoring User Agency in a World of Locked-Down Apps

**Authors:** Geoffrey Litt, Josh Horowitz, Peter van Hardenberg, Todd Matthews  
**Date:** June 2025  
**Source:** Ink & Switch

## Overview

This essay proposes "malleable software"—computing systems that users can adapt to their unique needs with minimal friction. The authors argue that current mass-produced applications are too rigid and that computing infrastructure should shift from treating users as passive consumers to empowering them as active co-creators.

## Core Argument

The essay begins with a fundamental observation: "Age plus adaptivity is what makes a building come to be loved" (quoting Stewart Brand). Physical environments naturally adapt to inhabitants' needs, but digital tools have largely become locked-down appliances controlled by distant corporations.

The authors illustrate this problem through examples like doctors forced to complete irrelevant fields in electronic medical records, and software teams losing process fluidity when switching from adaptable physical boards to rigid digital trackers.

## Three Design Patterns for Malleability

### 1. A Gentle Slope from User to Creator

Drawing on MacLean et al.'s research, the essay advocates for systems where customization power increases incrementally without steep learning cliffs. Spreadsheets exemplify this pattern—users can progress from viewing data to editing cells to modifying formulas naturally.

Key characteristics:
- "In-place toolchains" where editing tools are always available
- Explicit modes (like HyperCard's levels) that provide guardrails
- Multiple customization techniques at different complexity levels

### 2. Tools, Not Apps

The essay contrasts specialized applications (like avocado slicers) with general-purpose tools (like knives). Current app silos prevent data sharing and workflow composition. Solutions include:

- **Shared data:** Desktop filesystems and databases like Airtable enable multiple tools to operate on shared information
- **UI composition:** Systems like OpenDoc embedded multiple editors within documents, and Dynamicland explores physical arrangement as composition mechanism

### 3. Communal Creation

Malleability shouldn't be purely individualistic. Communities benefit from shared solutions—teams maintain their own customized software rather than adapting universal products. The essay cites the free software movement as inspiration, where "situated software" serves specific communities without needing global appeal.

## Existing Approaches and Their Limits

The essay evaluates common customization methods:

- **Settings:** Only expose pre-determined options
- **Plugins:** Limited to authorized extension points
- **Modding:** Permissionless but difficult to maintain; crosses chasm between installing and creating
- **Open source:** Access to code doesn't eliminate friction of setup and expertise
- **AI-assisted coding:** Generates new applications but doesn't address composability or control of existing tools

## Ink & Switch Prototypes

The authors describe research projects exploring malleable infrastructure:

**Infrastructure layer:**
- **PushPin:** Web-based collaborative canvas using "document functional reactive programming"
- **Cambria:** Live schema translation enabling tools to interoperate despite different data formats
- **Farm:** Hosting code as synchronized Automerge documents
- **Patchwork:** Adds version control and branching; the authors use it internally for their own work

**Dynamic documents layer:**
- **Potluck:** Text-based recipe planning enriched with interactive scaling and timers
- **Embark:** Travel planning using hierarchical outlines with embedded maps and weather integration

Key finding from Patchwork: "Opportunities for improving your software can emerge naturally within a malleable system." The authors created a word-counter tool mid-writing project, demonstrating how AI assistance combines effectively with malleable environments.

## Remaining Challenges

The essay acknowledges significant unresolved questions:

- **Privacy and security:** Balancing extensibility with protection against malicious modifications
- **Business models:** Sustainable funding for composable tools rather than monolithic applications
- **Cultural shifts:** Cultivating desire for digital agency and environmental adaptation

## Call to Action

The essay concludes by inviting different stakeholders to participate:

- Researchers should reimagine foundational computing metaphors
- Platform developers should see users as capable participants
- Software makers should empower customization rather than enforce uniformity

The ultimate vision: digital environments that evolve to reflect inhabitants' needs, developing character through use rather than imposed design consistency.
