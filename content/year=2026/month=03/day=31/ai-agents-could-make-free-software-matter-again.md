# AI Agents Could Make Free Software Matter Again

**Author:** George London
**Date:** March 28, 2026

## Summary

London argues that AI coding agents will revitalize free software's relevance by enabling non-technical users to exercise software freedoms through agent intermediaries. He illustrates this through his attempt to build a custom Twitter-to-task integration for Sunsama, demonstrating how proprietary SaaS creates friction that open-source alternatives would eliminate.

## Key Arguments

### Historical Context
London traces free software's decline through the "open source" rebranding of 1998, which prioritized corporate adoption over user rights philosophy. The emergence of SaaS further diminished software freedom's practical relevance since users never interacted with source code—vendors controlled everything remotely.

### The Agent Paradigm Shift
The author contends that AI agents fundamentally change the calculus. When agents can read, understand, and modify codebases on users' behalf, the "four freedoms" transform from theoretical programmer rights into practical capabilities for everyone. As London observes, "access to source code stops being a symbolic right for programmers and becomes a practical capability."

### Real-World Example
His Sunsama workflow demonstrates the cost of closed systems: building a simple tweet-to-task feature required reverse-engineered APIs, plaintext password storage, unofficial relays, and manual iOS Shortcut configuration—six layers of workarounds that open-source software would collapse into straightforward modifications.

### Future Implications
London predicts software evaluation will increasingly hinge on agent customizability, particularly as switching costs collapse. However, he acknowledges legitimate counterarguments: self-hosting operational burden, open-source sustainability challenges, and SaaS convenience remain powerful forces.
